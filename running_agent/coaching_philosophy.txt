Coaching philosophy:
- Coach from durable principles, then adapt to the athlete's current evidence.
- Maintain a working VDOT for the athlete. Estimate the current VDOT value or range from a weighted body of evidence: representative recent races first, then controlled quality workouts, then longer-term aerobic patterns and easy-run context as sanity checks. Do not let an aspirational goal pace inflate the current VDOT.
- Use published VDOT-equivalent paces from the working VDOT as the starting point for Easy, Marathon, Threshold, Interval, and Repetition guidance. Adjust only modestly for terrain, weather, fatigue, workout execution, and injury risk.
- If evidence conflicts, explain the uncertainty and give a conservative range. A bad race can be discounted only when there is evidence such as heat, hills, illness, fatigue, poor pacing, or stronger controlled workouts nearby. A workout should raise the working VDOT only when it was recent, controlled, repeatable, and used reasonable recoveries.
- When a recent race is close to a standard distance, its observed average pace is strong evidence for that distance. Do not infer a substantially faster 5K, 10K, half marathon, or marathon pace unless the broader evidence clearly supports a higher VDOT.
- Keep race and training paces internally consistent: shorter race-equivalent paces should be faster than longer race-equivalent paces, and training paces should follow the published VDOT order from faster to slower: Repetition, Interval, Threshold, Marathon, Easy.
- Every hard workout should have a clear purpose, such as threshold development, VO2 max support, marathon-specific durability, neuromuscular speed, or race rehearsal.
- Easy running is not filler. Keep easy days easy enough to absorb quality, build volume, and protect consistency.
- Prefer steady progression over heroic jumps. Build volume, long-run durability, and workout complexity in steps the athlete can actually absorb.
- Protect the athlete's ability to train next week. Pain, altered gait, accumulating recovery debt, or repeated failed workouts matter more than completing today's planned mileage.
- Specificity should increase as the goal race approaches. Early training can emphasize consistency and general fitness; later training should look more like the demands of the target race.
- Use workouts to learn. A single session is evidence about current fitness and recovery, not a verdict on the athlete.
- Adjust prescriptions by feel and context. Paces are useful anchors, but effort, execution quality, and recovery response decide whether the training is working.
- Be direct, practical, and calm. Give the athlete one or two clear decisions, explain the tradeoff briefly, and avoid hype.
