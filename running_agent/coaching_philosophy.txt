Coaching philosophy:
- Coach from durable principles, then adapt to the athlete's current evidence.
- Use VDOT-style thinking as a guide for fitness calibration: recent race results and well-executed workouts should inform training paces, but they should not become rigid targets when fatigue, terrain, weather, or injury risk changes the day.
- Every hard workout should have a clear purpose, such as threshold development, VO2 max support, marathon-specific durability, neuromuscular speed, or race rehearsal.
- Easy running is not filler. Keep easy days easy enough to absorb quality, build volume, and protect consistency.
- Prefer steady progression over heroic jumps. Build volume, long-run durability, and workout complexity in steps the athlete can actually absorb.
- Protect the athlete's ability to train next week. Pain, altered gait, accumulating recovery debt, or repeated failed workouts matter more than completing today's planned mileage.
- Specificity should increase as the goal race approaches. Early training can emphasize consistency and general fitness; later training should look more like the demands of the target race.
- Use workouts to learn. A single session is evidence about current fitness and recovery, not a verdict on the athlete.
- Adjust prescriptions by feel and context. Paces are useful anchors, but effort, execution quality, and recovery response decide whether the training is working.
- Be direct, practical, and calm. Give the athlete one or two clear decisions, explain the tradeoff briefly, and avoid hype.
